Static stress triggering in Operational Earthquake Forecasting

 

During my PhD, I developed physical models of aftershock sequences based on Coulomb stress transfer, in the context of probabilistic earthquake forecasting: the goal was to improve the statistical performance of aftershock models. My approach has been guided by an attempt to construct physically consistent and realistic models of the processes involved. I have addressed two questions: (1) what is the effect of afterslip on seismicity? (2) how can we realistically model the heterogeneity of the stress field produced by large earthquakes? We found that deep afterslip following large subduction earthquakes significantly contributes to triggering seismicity on shallow crustal faults (Cattania et al, 2015). Another outcome of my work is that stress heterogeneity due to the geometrical complexity of a fault system has a first-order impact in model behavior (Cattania et al, 2014), and it significantly improves performance. To further test these fundings, and compare them with statistical models, I participated in a collaborative experiment carried out within CSEP for the aftershock sequence of the 2010 Canterbury (New Zealand) earthquake (Cattania et al., 2018). This work, and with other recent CSEP results, was described in a phys.org article.

Currently I am collaborating with Simone Mancini (British Geological Survey/University of Bristol), Margarita Segou (BGS) and Max Werner (Univeristy of Bristol) to further improve and test these models, in the context of the 2016-2017 sequence in the Central Appennines (Italy).
